GDPR Case Study - The Dublin Mint Office Limited

INITIAL POST

The General data protection regulation (GDPR) is a set of privacy laws, introduced in May 2018. GDPR comprises a wide range of privacy standards, these are designed to protect personal data and the regulation is specific to the European Union (EU). Companies are now required to build privacy settings into their digital landscape. As the new laws are now encapsulated in a legally binding regulation, there is no option to simply ignore or opt out (MacDonald, 2021).

This case study highlights October 2017 when the Dublin mint office marketing department made a phone call to an individual and his son trying to sell them a commemorative coin. A complaint was lodged to the data protection commission as both father and son whom registered online, had selected the marketing opt-out box. During the investigation the mint office admitted that by opting out of marketing, the selection was opting out of email and not out of telesales (DPC.ie). The specific aspect of GDPR that was addressed in this case was Data Permission (MacDonald, 2021). As there was no tick box to opt-out of telesales which means, consent was not freely given.

As the Dublin Mint had been given a previous warning for a similar complaint, they were prosecuted by the DPC in May 2018. However, the court applied Section 1(1) of the probation of Offenders Act which meant that there was no conviction or fine (eISB 1907). The Dublin Mint office apologized in writing to the complainant and agreed to pay for the DCPâ€™s prosecution costs.

Based on the reading to mitigate such data breaches in future implementing an in depth investigation into the marketing department, perform a gap analysis and enforce changes to be GPR compliant.
