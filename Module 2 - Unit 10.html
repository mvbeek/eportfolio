Module 2 - Unit 10

Network and Information Security Management

Analysing and Mitigating Security Breaches

Provide a summary of the discussion based on your initial post, the feedback from your peers and the content of all 3 units.

SUMMARY POST

My initial post discussed the General data protection regulation (GDPR) and how it encompasses a wide range of privacy Standards designed to protect personal data, specific to the European Union (EU). The post discusses how the marketing department of the Dublin mint office was in violation of GDPR. They were taken to court by the Data Protection Commission (DPC) for contacting individuals via telephone, even though the Individuals had opted out of marketing upon registering online. As there was no option to opt out of telesales, consent to be contacted was not freely given. The specific aspect of GDPR that was addressed in this case was Data Permissions (MacDonald, 2021).

There were some fantastic points made in the peer responses. For example, Beran mentioned that fines should be imposed on companies to change the approach towards GDPR regulations, thus insuring the privacy rights of individuals. While in principle I do agree with Beran, this case was slightly different however as the court applied Section 1(1) of the probation of offenders Act, which meant there was no conviction or fine (eISB 1907).

Mahamad suggests that the Dublin Mint office should perform a root cause analysis to figure out how to prevent such incidents from occurring in the future. Root cause analysis is an approach used to analyze serious problems before trying to solve them, by which the main root cause of a problem is isolated and identified (SixSigma, 2017). If the mint were to have performed such an analysis they could have worked to avoid and mitigate the results of such serious failures in the future.

Muhammad asked how organizations whose business relies on such marketing calls are safe from possible prosecutions? Great question, according to GDPR as long as your customers give consent and you as a business are able to prove that, marketing calls are then permitted (Rapcsák, 2018).

In conclusion the establishing of GDPR appears to have been a great initiative and now acts as a vital tool in protecting the privacy rights of individuals. Although, in this case, the Dublin Mint was not fined for their violation of GDPR, many companies have subsequently been fined and prosecuted. To date there has been 874 GDPR prosecution fines to the value of over 1 billion euros (enforcementtracker, 2021).
