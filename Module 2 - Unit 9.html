Module 2 - Unit 9

Network and Information Security Management

System Logging and Forensics

Discuss two of the most important techniques used in security incident investigations: logging and forensics. Logging will cover the tools and techniques used in auditing actions performed on a system. Forensics deals with the tools and techniques used to investigate and preserve evidence of a breach and is often subject to legal controls.

Initial Post: Loss of sensitive personal data contained in an evidence file

In this case, The Data Protection Commission addresses a complaint regarding the loss of sensitive personal information contained in an evidence file while in the possession of AGS (An Garda Síochána). 

The investigation that took place, the officer of AGS was disciplined for not handling the client's sensitive data securely as one of the requirements of the data protection act is that the data handler is obligated to securely and properly store all Personally Identifiable Information.

For organizations such as AGS who deal with client's sensitive information, it is highly recommended to secure the receival of information from the very start.

Some examples of securing the process are below:

1. A process should be created so all clients are required to follow if they (the clients) need to send sensitive information, such as encryption of data while stored on the portable media (CD, DVD, etc).
2. The encrypted data should be sent using authorized courier who specialize on handling/moving sensitive information.
3. Decryption key or the password to unlock the encrypted media should be transferred using another mean such as providing over a phone call after verification of the package delivery.
4. Once the organization receives the sensitive data, the data must be stored in monitored and secured offices and lockers.
5. Properly enforced guidelines as a written checklist for staff that handles sensitive information to ensure all necessary steps are followed.
6. Mandatory and regular training of staffs who handle sensitive information to keep them focused and up to date.

The criminal justice sector in the UK has holds a wide range of sensitive information about regarding its citizens. However, UK law enforcement is somewhat exempt from GDPR’s scope, in certain circumstances when investigating a crime (ICO.org 2021). Interestingly there have been several law enforcement cases investigated by the Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO). Some examples of this are outlined by (Conner, 2020) below;

- The Greater Manchester Police was being fined £150,000 after three DVDs containing footage of interviews with victims of violent or sexual crimes got lost in the post.

- Scotland Yard breached breaching data protection laws to spy on one of its officers while she was on sick leave.

- The Ministry of Justice was being fined £180,000 for “serious failings” in the handling of confidential data following the loss of a hard drive containing the details of almost 3,000 prisoners at Erlestoke prison in Wiltshire.

When considered further it is apparent that there is a vital requirement for all, including nations own law enforcement, to be compliant with an international set of standardised regulations?? governing the rights of conglomerates, smaller corporations, and individual civilians across the globe. Cyber security feeds into ethical governance both on a corporate and governmental level. It has never held a more vital role in society than it does in today’s fully digitalised world.

Initial Post

Few Years ago Facebook gave unauthorized and unrestricted access to personally identifiable information of more than 87 million users to Cambridge Analytica (a British consulting firm). This accident raised the alarm to a crucial concern of data privacy among politicians, researchers, and technologists in which they must be involved to develop policies that protect the privacy of individuals using applications, websites, and online services. (Isaak & HANNA, 2018)

In a similar case of information disclosure, a solicitors for a resident of a direct provision center submitted a compliant, that there was a disclosure of CCTV footage. The complainant stated that his personal data have been leaked to a radio program in a form of image. The accident came to attention when the radio host claimed that he had a copy of a CCTV footage of the complainant in an altercation with another resident which was taken from a room inside the accommodation center.

The accommodation center is owned by the state, under the responsibility of RIA (Reception and Integration Agency) and it is considered as controller and managed by Aramark Ireland (A data processor which processing data of behalf of RIA). The data protection commission (DPC) investigated the case and came to find that there was not an agreement of data processing between the RIA and Aramark, which forms a contravention of section 2C(3) of data protection Acts 1988 and 2003 by RIA as the data controller.  

Also, as a violation of complainant’s right of privacy, the DPC stated that both RIA and Aramark failed to meet the appropriate security measures against unauthorized disclosure, moreover, a contravention against 2C(2) had occurred, which makes RIA as data controller to be responsible of developing security measures to comply by the employees.  

For organizations, it is an essential step to develop policies that govern the way tasks are performed, and procedures which provide a blueprint on how to perform tasks. Such disclosures are preventable in case of the existence of policies and procedures. (Peltier, 2004)

Mahamad, an interesting post regarding Closed Circuit Television (CCTV) footage being leaked. Of particular interest is that video footage is considered as controller and therefor falls under GDPR. Your first paragraph mentions Facebook, although it has not been made clear how this relates to CCTV footage? You also mention that there was no agreement between RIA and Aramark, what does that mean for the outcome of the case?

Interestingly, one of the first penalties issued regarding the use of CCTV under GDPR was against an Austrian retailer (Irwin, 2021). The Austrian Data Protection Authority (DPA) fined a retail company 4,800 euros for monitoring a public space without proper transparency and notice. It was considered that this CCTV surveillance was capturing too much of the sidewalk (Fritz, 2018) despite this ruling the awarded fine was minuscule compared some other fines issued in the UK. By contrast, for example, The Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO) has previously issued fines totaling £282 million against British Airways and Marriott International for GDPR violations (Irwin, 2021).

The above all goes to show the importance for all cyber security professionals, in maintaining an awareness of local laws and regulations that companies fall under, in which parts of the world they are enforced and in what ways. In my view one part of compliance is maintaining a basic level simple corporate social responsibility whilst another is covering your own job security by ensuring your clients personal and professional data is always safely secure.
