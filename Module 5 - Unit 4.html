Module 5 - Unit 4

Usable Security Design 


This week’s seminar will focus on planning a usable security design project. Attention will be given to evaluating approaches and identifying the best or a combination of methods to ensure a suitable method is adopted to address the problem. This will involve you applying the knowledge from Unit 3 to carry out a task based on a case study.

In this unit we shall:
- Explore the various techniques to consider when planning a usable design solution.
- Discuss the strengths and weakness of the techniques and their application contexts.
- Apply the contextual design approach to a case study.

On completion of this unit you will be able to:
- Effectively plan a usable security design solution.
- Appraise critically the techniques and their applications to the design process.
- Develop the ability to evaluate a usable design solution.


PEER REVIEW

You will respond to 2 of your peers' viewpoints by the end of Unit 4. You need to complete two 200-word reviews, one for each essay. The total word count for this assignment is 400 words.

Review 1 – 1793854618 (198 words)

An engaging essay, your opening line referencing the robot grabbing and killing a worker is quite a statement and makes one interested to read further. The section pertaining some prominent human factors involved in cyber threats was particularly compelling. However, one critique being that perhaps the sentences are a little too long for easy reading and there is a notable lack of references to support the statements being made. Another critique being that the reference regarding the robot is not listed below the essay. Perhaps a more comprehensive approach would be to analyze the cyber threats alongside the implications of applying an ASMIS system?

Interestingly, having implemented ASMIS, one implication is Queens Medical Centre thereby storing sensitive personal identifiable information, thus be obligated to follow the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). Given the growing number of cyber-attacks in the healthcare industry, the practice will be required to share how they will use data collected, in compliance with this new legislation (GDPR, 2018). NSTC (2016) state that the human factor is a scientific field that is underutilized and undervalued in information security and cybersecurity. Perhaps more research and understanding of human behaviour in cyber security is the way forward.

Review 2 – 1795321420 (200 words)

An interesting essay, you list a good number of points. Of particular interest is the mention of some human factors and their implications on said information systems. My only critique would be that there is no clear answer to the concerns management have around the government’s policy on patient data protection. What are your thoughts on data protection surrounding the ASMIS system, with a focus of course specifically on health care?

It seems digital transformation and the COVID-19 global pandemic have expedited the adoption rate of health information systems, to the detriment of human factors (Nifakos et al, 2021). Due to the private nature and accumulation of private healthcare data, one can imagine why this information is sought out by cyber criminals (Argaw, et al. 2020). Queens Medical Centre is based in the UK and therefore falls under the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) implemented in 2018. This act requires the Healthcare Sector to prove how their data is secured, used, and stored, requiring prior consent (GDPR, 2018). It is apparent that this sector of industry deals very much with the emotional and physical safety of individuals, therefore being complaint with data protection regulations goes beyond geographical boundaries (TrendMicro, 2018).
