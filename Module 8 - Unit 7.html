Validity and Generalisability in Research


A look at the validity, generalisability and reliability. These are three important dimensions within research as they affect any conclusions you can make after your data collection and analysis. All these need to be considered before you collect any data.

You will then look at the differences in qualitative data and quantitative data and what you can do with it: how to analyse your data and how to present your results should be considered before you collect any data. Note that quantitative data leads us into descriptive and inferential statistics. Before this, however, there are processes of data cleansing and validation that need to be considered.

In this unit we shall:

- Introduce the concepts of validity, generalisability and reliability and how they impact on your research design.
- Consider the differences between qualitative and quantitative data and how to analyse and present your results.

On completion of this unit you will be able to:

- Understand how the concepts of validity, generalisability and reliability affect your investigation and the design   of your research method.
- Consider how to analyse and present the results you obtain from your investigation and how they will enable you to answer your research question.

Validity and Generalisability in ResearchURL

Internal Validity

Answering this question will inevitably involve a degree of subjective judgement, but by managing the various threats to the validity of our research we can improve our chances of producing valid work.

A study is internally valid if it describes the true state of affairs within its own setting.

Interpretative Validity

This mainly relates to the analysis of qualitative data but could be applied to quantitative analysis.

It comes down to the interpretation of the data and the results; who is doing the interpretation, and do they have a hidden agenda.

Generalisability (External Validity)

Generalisability looks at whether findings from a particular research study are applicable in other research settings. This is something that is considered particularly in research studies involving a relatively wide sample (e.g., questionnaire).

Unbiasedness and Precision
Ideally, we want any measurement taken from our sample to be unbiased. In other words, to be representative of the true population value.

Bias can come into measurements in many ways, but choosing a random sample is one way to avoid this.
If our measurements are precise, then we mean they are repeatable. So, if another investigation is undertaken using the same methods, they should obtain similar results.

Ideally, our sample values are both unbiased and precise.
An estimate of a parameter taken from a random sample is known to be unbiased.
As the sample size increases, it becomes more precise.

Random Samples

Often a population will contain too many individuals to study easily so we tend to use a sample. A well-chosen sample will contain most of the information about a particular population characteristic. The sample should be representative of the population, that is, the members of the sample should have similar characteristics to the population.

To avoid bias in the selection of the sample a random sample is chosen. This ensures that everyone in the population has an equal chance of being chosen. The word "random" describes the way in which it is selected. Approaching people in the street, or in a supermarket, to answer your questionnaire is not a random sample as not everyone in your population of interest has an equal chance of being selected. Also, self-selecting samples, mainly from online surveys, is not a random sample and is more likely to be biased than any sample. This is because people with a particular view are more likely to engage.

Qualitative research tends to be not only about “what” people think but also “why”.

Qualitative research methods originated in the social and behavioural sciences. Today our world is more complex and so it becomes more difficult to understand what people think and perceive.

Content analysis is a widely accepted, and the most frequently employed, technique. It can be used to analyse the information from text and images.

Narrative analysis is used to analyse content gathered from various sources such as personal interviews, field observation and surveys.  

Discourse analysis is similar to narrative analysis and is used to analyse the interactions with people.

Grounded theory is used when you want to explain why a particular phenomenon happened.

Discussion Topic
Case Study: Accuracy of information

Abi is a researcher at an institute and also a statistical programmer. Abi has received a project from a manufacturer to review the nutritional value of a new cereal, Whizzz. Having collected the necessary data, he now needs to perform the appropriate analyses and print the reports for him to send to the manufacturer. Unfortunately, the data Abi has collected seems to refute the claim that Whizzz is nutritious, and, in fact, they may indicate that Whizzz is harmful.

Abi also realises that some other correlations could be performed that would cast Whizzz in a more favourable light. “After all,” he thinks, “I can use statistics to support either side of any issue.”

Ethical Concerns

- Clearly, if Abi changed data values in this study he would be acting unethically. But is it any more ethical for him to suggest analysing correct data in a way that supports two or more different conclusions?
- Is Abi obligated to present both the positive and the negative analyses?
- Is Abi responsible for the use to which others put his program results?
- If Abi does put forward both sets of results to the manufacturer, he suspects that they will publicise only the positive ones. What other courses of action has he?

You should also highlight legal, social and professional impacts of any choices made. Please note that there are no right or wrong answers here and you may introduce local, as well as international, legislature in your responses.

INITIAL POST

In the research and design context, ethics is concerned with the moral issues arising from the research activities, outcomes, and conduct of the researcher. The scaffolding of ethical research discussions results from notorious unethical research practices traced to the 16th century. The disclosures of bioethical scandals like the Guatemala STD studies and the cultivation of the HeLa cell in the US escalated the need to develop measures to enhance ethical governance in research (Knijnenburg et al., 2022). Stringent measures were required to guarantee that research does not unduly harm humans, animals, and the environment. Recent scandals like Cambridge Analytica have raised considerable ethical concerns about data privacy, especially in computing.

Weinbaum et al. (2019) critically analysed two hundred popular peer-reviewed articles on scientific research ethics. The comprehensive analysis reveals the commonalities and differences in ethical considerations across diverse scientific disciplines. Ethical principles primarily apply to ethical scientific inquiry, researchers' ethical conduct, and participants' treatment. The researchers identified ten ethical principles cutting across scientific and technical disciplines.

Figure 1 (Below) shows the categories of the ethical principles of scientific research. Various principles of ethical scientific research apply to the case study on the accuracy of information. Abi is a researcher, and statistical programmer tasked with a noble scientific inquiry to review the nutritional value of Whizz, a new cereal.

The first ethical concern is the ethicality of Abi's action to analyse correct data to support different conclusions. According to the principle of integrity, researchers must demonstrate honesty and truthfulness when executing scientific inquiries (Weinbaum et al., 2019). Scientific researchers must not fabricate data, omit relevant data, or falsify results. Therefore, Abi will be deemed unethical if the differing conclusions do not benefit society.

The second ethical concern regards whether Abi is obligated to present both positive and negative analyses. The principle of non-discrimination states that research should minimise attempts to narrow research benefits to specific groups (Weinbaum et al., 2019). Abi is not obligated to provide both analyses, but the duty to society demands that scientific research should contribute to the well-being of society. Therefore, if providing both positive and negative analyses will be beneficial, Abi should proceed.

Moreover, the issue of whether Abi is responsible for the use of the results is a complex ethical concern. The principle of professional competence demands scientific researchers to engage in work they are qualified to perform. Professional discipline and integrity principles also highlight the need for researchers to be truthful and honest (Weinbaum et al., 2019). Though Abi may not be held responsible for the results, the results should benefit society. According to the principle of beneficence, research must strive for benefits to outweigh the risks.

The final ethical concern is the recommended course of action if the manufacturer only publishes positive results. The principle of conflict of interest applies to this issue. Scientific research should minimise issues that could lead to biased results (Knijnenburg et al., 2022). As an ethical researcher and professional, Abi can take appropriate legal action to ensure that the manufacturer does use the results discriminatively. The manufacturer is liable for the legal, social, and professional implications of using the results. De Boer (2021) asserts that omitting some nutritional information violates the European Regulation on food information to consumers (FIC) and relevant agencies in the UK. Consequently, the results of scientific research must be used correctly and ethically.
