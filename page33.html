Lecture on Risk BC and DR

Business continuity (BC) and disaster recovery (DR) are intrinsically linked– and in most scenarios DR is considered a subset of BC. The former may not be security driven – for example the 2020 worldwide pandemic caused by Covid-19 led to many businesses invoking their BC plans to cope with government mandated shutdown to keep their business' working by allowing many staff to work from home. However, although the invocation was not security related, the change in business activities had a major impact on a businesses security, risks, and policies.

For example when staff work from home their employers need to ensure that the equipment they use is up to date with security patches; that they have a secure method of accessing business documents; that any documents stored locally are also held securely (i.e. by the use of encryption); that their home IT infrastructure is secure and has not been compromised (e.g. do they have a firewall? Is it patched up to date? Do they have any unusual configuration settings, etc). Some of these questions can be managed by technology, others may need policy and procedure changes.

Both BC and DR plans are driven by business requirements and should form part of an overall enterprise architecture design, including the security architecture. One of the most commonly used architecture frameworks is TOGAF (the Open Group Architecture Framework). The architecture development method (ADM) together with the modifications due to the SABSA (Sherwood Applied Business Security Architecture) model. The reading this week focuses on background material around BC and DR planning, as well as several papers covering various areas of research that are driving the future of Information Risk Management.

In particular you should read the article by Aven (2016) that reviews risk assessment and risk management and future trends in the field.
